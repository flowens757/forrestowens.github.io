--- layout: archive title: "Research" permalink: /research/ author_profile: true --- My research is at the intersection of comparative politics, international relations, political economy, and environmental politics. I study how climate and environmental change shape political behavior in developing countries, especially across Africa. ## Research Interests: Climate politics; political economy; African politics; international development; foreign aid; public opinion. ##Dissertation: Climate Shocks and Political Behavior in Africa## The impact of climate and environmental change on political systems is understudied in political science. Climate shocks occur in all countries and impact almost all aspects of everyday life, from the physical health and economic security of individuals to constraints on infrastructure and public good provision, yet we know little about how individuals and politicians respond. Some attention is given to environmental and climate change in developed countries, yet considerably less attention is given to developing countries. Developing countries, however, are unique in their political institutions and therefore may respond to environmental stress quite differently than developed countries. In each of my chapters, I focus on various aspects of political behavior, protest, vote buying, and political attitudes to address how climate shocks influence political behavior. ## Working Papers ## **Climate Shocks and Protest in Nigeria** This paper investigates how climate shocks influence protest behavior in Nigeria from 1997-2024. Canonical theories of protest indicate that protests are most likely to occur in well-populated, urban areas, yet more rural, agrarian areas are the most vulnerable to climate shocks and therefore may be more likely to protest after experiencing extreme heat or variable rainfall. Using protest data from the Armed Conflict Location Dataset (ACLED) and high-resolution temperature and precipitation data, I examine how climate shocks influence the amount of protest in Nigeria. I find that temperature shocks increase expected protest count only when they occur in agrarian communities, especially during the growing season. This finding is suggestive of a livelihood degradation mechanism where climate shocks increase food insecurity while simultaneously decreasing future income in agrarian areas. Shocks in agrarian communities then create a temporary window for protest in areas that traditionally are less likely to experience protest. **Climate Shocks and Preferences** for Taxation and Public Services in Africa** How do climate shocks shape public support for taxation and government services in developing countries? Existing political economy theories suggest that increased economic risk should raise demand for redistribution and public goods provision, yet this relationship may not hold in contexts characterized by weak state capacity and low trust. Using Afrobarometer Round 8 data merged with high-resolution temperature and precipitation shocks, we examine how climate variability influences tax-service preferences across Africa. We find a dynamic temporal pattern: temperature shocks in the short term (0–3 months) significantly reduce support for higher taxes and expanded public services, consistent with immediate liquidity constraints, while shocks in the medium term (4–6 months) increase support, suggesting that individuals update their preferences as the economic consequences of shocks materialize. However, these effects are heterogeneous. Farmers and more deprived individuals are more likely to support less taxation following recent shocks. These findings challenge standard risk-based theories of redistribution by showing that in weak-state contexts, heightened vulnerability does not necessarily translate into greater demand for government intervention, highlighting the importance of state capacity and trust in shaping political responses to climate change. **Climate Shocks and Vote Buying in Africa** This project examines whether climate shocks alter the incentives and opportunities for clientelistic political exchange. **Climate Change Opinion and Political Accountability** This project asks how climate exposure and political context shape public opinion about climate change and government responsibility.
