--- layout: single title: "Research" permalink: /research/ author_profile: true --- My research is at the intersection of comparative politics, international relations, political economy, and environmental politics. I study how climate and environmental change shape political behavior in developing countries, especially across Africa. My work is motivated by an overarching interest in how changing environmental conditions influence the interactions between individuals, their domestic government, and other states. Working Papers

  • Automated Survey Harmonization via Source-Consistent Entity Resolution. Under review. With Dawson Verley, Sara Constantino, Brandon Dela Cuesta, and Amanda Kennard.
    Abstract

    Survey harmonization is a common challenge in applied social science research, particularly for multi-wave surveys in which question wording varies across rounds. Existing approaches rely on manual coding, making them difficult to scale and reproduce. We develop a fully automated method by framing survey harmonization as a problem of multi-source entity resolution. The approach combines sentence-embedding similarity with a source-constrained agglomerative clustering algorithm that enforces a one-per-source matching rule. Evaluated on Afrobarometer data, the method achieves a precision of 0.941 and a recall of 0.969 (F1 = 0.955), closely matching human-coded results while operating at substantially lower cost. Applied to Latinobarometro, the method performs comparably, achieving a precision of 0.945, a recall of 0.999, and an F1 of 0.971. The procedure scales efficiently to thousands of items and enables the construction of harmonized indices across survey rounds. More broadly, the framework applies to data integration problems involving multiple sources without reliable identifiers.

  • Climate Shocks and Protest in Nigeria.
    Abstract

    This paper investigates how climate shocks influence protest behavior in Nigeria from 1997-2024. Canonical theories of protest indicate that protests are most likely to occur in well-populated, urban areas, yet more rural, agrarian areas are the most vulnerable to climate shocks and therefore may be more likely to protest after experiencing extreme heat or variable rainfall. Using protest data from the Armed Conflict Location Dataset (ACLED) and high-resolution temperature and precipitation data, I examine how climate shocks influence the amount of protest in Nigeria. I find that temperature shocks increase expected protest count only when they occur in agrarian communities, especially during the growing season. This finding is suggestive of a livelihood degradation mechanism where climate shocks increase food insecurity while simultaneously decreasing future income in agrarian areas. Shocks in agrarian communities then create a temporary window for protest in areas that traditionally are less likely to experience protest.

  • Climate Shocks and Preferences for Taxation and Public Services in Africa. With Sara Constantino, Brandon De la Cuesta, and Amanda Kennard.
    Abstract

    How do climate shocks shape public support for taxation and government services in developing countries? Existing political economy theories suggest that increased economic risk should raise demand for redistribution and public goods provision, yet this relationship may not hold in contexts characterized by weak state capacity and low trust. Using Afrobarometer Round 8 data merged with high-resolution temperature and precipitation shocks, we examine how climate variability influences tax-service preferences across Africa. We find a dynamic temporal pattern: temperature shocks in the short term, 0-3 months, significantly reduce support for higher taxes and expanded public services, consistent with immediate liquidity constraints, while shocks in the medium term, 4-6 months, increase support, suggesting that individuals update their preferences as the economic consequences of shocks materialize. However, these effects are heterogeneous. Farmers and more deprived individuals are more likely to support less taxation following recent shocks. These findings challenge standard risk-based theories of redistribution by showing that in weak-state contexts, heightened vulnerability does not necessarily translate into greater demand for government intervention, highlighting the importance of state capacity and trust in shaping political responses to climate change.

  • Rewarding Ambitious Climate Pledges. With Gabriella Montinola.
    Abstract

    This article examines whether the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) states are rewarding developing countries for submitting more ambitious carbon mitigation pledges to slow the impact of climate change as a part of the Paris Climate Accords (COP21). It contributes to the overall literature on aid allocation in general and provides much-needed nuance to the understudied relationship between aid and the environment. Using aid, climate, and governance data, we analyze aid commitments from 2013-2020. The results show that there is a distinction between aid allocations in the aggregate and aid from individual donors. As a group, Development Assistance Committee (DAC) donors appear to give less aid to countries with ambitious climate pledges. However, there is significant variation in aid commitments from individual donors indicating that rewarding climate policies could be related to factors within donor countries. The findings also give support to both the need-based and merit-based aid literature as countries that are more vulnerable to climate change and those more prepared for climate change have greater aid commitments allocated to them. Curiously there is no relationship between ambitious climate pledges and environmental aid in particular. We conclude with various interpretations of these findings.
